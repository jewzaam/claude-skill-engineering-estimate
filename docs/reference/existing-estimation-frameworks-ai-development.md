# Existing Estimation Frameworks for AI-Assisted Development

> Research compiled: 2026-03-20
> Generated By: Claude Code (claude-opus-4-6)

## TL;DR

No standardized estimation framework exists specifically for AI-assisted development. The field is fragmented across academic research (LLM-based effort estimation agents), practitioner adaptations (modified story points, AI augmentation factors for COCOMO), and emerging measurement frameworks (DORA AI Capabilities Model, DX AI Measurement Framework). The most significant finding is the METR study demonstrating that developers consistently overestimate AI's productivity impact -- predicting 24% speedup but actually experiencing 19% slowdown on complex tasks. Addy Osmani's "70% Problem" provides the most widely-cited practitioner framework for understanding effort distribution in AI-assisted workflows.

## Source 1: METR Randomized Controlled Trial (2025)

- **Title:** "Measuring the Impact of Early-2025 AI on Experienced Open-Source Developer Productivity"
- **Authors:** Becker, Rush, et al. (METR)
- **Publication Date:** July 10, 2025
- **URL (successfully retrieved via search):** https://metr.org/blog/2025-07-10-early-2025-ai-experienced-os-dev-study/
- **ArXiv:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.09089

### Key Findings

- **Study Design:** Randomized controlled trial with 16 experienced developers completing 246 tasks on their own mature open-source repositories (average 5 years experience, 1,500 commits per developer). Tasks randomly assigned to allow or disallow AI tools. Developers used Cursor Pro with Claude 3.5/3.7 Sonnet.
- **Estimation vs. Reality:**
  - Before starting: developers predicted AI would reduce completion time by **24%**
  - After completing: developers estimated AI reduced completion time by **20%**
  - Actual measured result: AI **increased** completion time by **19%** (i.e., developers were slower)
  - External experts from economics predicted -39% and ML experts predicted -38% (both wrong)
- **Five causes of slowdown identified:** over-optimism about AI capabilities, high developer familiarity with their codebases, implicit/tacit knowledge that AI cannot access, large and complex codebases (avg 1.1M+ lines of code, 10+ years old), low AI reliability
- **Behavioral finding:** Screen recordings showed AI-assisted coding had more idle time -- not just model wait time, but periods of no activity at all
- **Post-study behavior:** 69% of developers continued using AI tooling after the study ended, suggesting AI reduces perceived cognitive effort even when it doesn't reduce time

### Relevance to Estimation

This is the strongest evidence that **developer self-estimates of AI productivity gains are unreliable** and should not be used as inputs to estimation models. Any estimation framework must account for this perception gap.

### Caveats

- Small sample size (16 developers)
- Focused on experienced developers on their own mature projects -- results may differ for junior developers or greenfield projects
- Used early-2025 frontier models; newer models may perform differently
- Study measured task completion time, not broader project delivery metrics

## Source 2: DORA 2025 State of AI-Assisted Software Development

- **Title:** "2025 DORA Report: State of AI-Assisted Software Development"
- **Authors:** DORA team (Google Cloud)
- **Publication Date:** 2025
- **URL:** https://dora.dev/research/2025/dora-report/
- **AI Capabilities Model:** https://dora.dev/ai/
- **InfoQ coverage:** https://www.infoq.com/news/2026/03/ai-dora-report/

### Framework: DORA AI Capabilities Model

Seven foundational capabilities that determine whether AI amplifies strengths or dysfunctions:

1. **Clear and communicated AI stance** -- explicit organizational policies on AI tool usage
2. **Healthy data ecosystems** -- high-quality, accessible, unified internal data
3. **AI-accessible internal data** -- connecting AI tools to internal documentation, codebases, decision logs
4. **Strong version control practices** -- mature habits become more critical with AI-accelerated code generation
5. **Working in small batches** -- especially valuable for AI-assisted teams
6. **User-centric focus** -- teams without user focus experience *negative* impacts from AI adoption
7. **Value Stream Management (VSM)** -- ensures AI is applied to actual constraints, not already-fast processes

### Key Finding: AI as Amplifier

AI does not automatically improve software delivery performance. It amplifies existing organizational capabilities -- making high-performing teams better and struggling teams worse.

### Estimation Implications

- AI productivity gains are conditional on organizational maturity
- Estimation must factor in organizational capability level, not just task characteristics
- Platform maturity is prerequisite: 90% of organizations have adopted at least one platform; quality of internal platform directly correlates with ability to unlock AI value
- Based on ~5,000 technology professionals and 100+ hours of qualitative data

### Caveats

- Survey-based methodology (self-reported data)
- Does not provide specific estimation formulas or scales
- Framework is organizational-diagnostic, not task-estimation

## Source 3: Addy Osmani -- "The 70% Problem" and "Beyond Vibe Coding"

- **Author:** Addy Osmani (Senior Engineering Leader, Google)
- **Publication:** Substack newsletter + O'Reilly book
- **70% Problem article (Dec 2024):** https://addyo.substack.com/p/the-70-problem-hard-truths-about
- **80% Problem update (Jan 2026):** https://addyo.substack.com/p/the-80-problem-in-agentic-coding
- **Book:** "Beyond Vibe Coding: From Coder to AI-Era Developer" (O'Reilly, 2026) -- https://beyond.addy.ie/

### Framework: The 70/30 Effort Split

The most widely-cited practitioner model for AI-assisted development effort distribution:

- AI can rapidly produce **70% of a solution** (scaffolding, obvious patterns)
- The remaining **30%** -- edge cases, security, production integration, debugging -- "can be just as time consuming as it ever was"
- In January 2026 update: the split shifted to approximately 80/20 for early adopters, but "the gap between prototype and production-ready software" has not closed

### Estimation Guidance

- **Invert traditional effort allocation:** Spend 70% of effort on problem definition, 30% on execution
- Write comprehensive specs, define success criteria, provide test cases up front
- "Code review is becoming the new bottleneck"
- Actual developer productivity measured at "1X, 2X. Maybe they can complete 20% more tasks than they could before"

### AI-Assisted Development Spectrum

Osmani distinguishes between:
- **Vibe coding** -- not reading AI code before running it (prototyping only)
- **AI-assisted engineering** -- structured approach combining AI creativity with engineering rigor: specs, review, collaboration

### Caveats

- Practitioner observations, not peer-reviewed research
- Percentages (70%, 80%) are approximate heuristics, not measured values
- Based primarily on Google's engineering context

## Source 4: Pavlic et al. -- LLMs for Agile Effort Estimation (MDPI 2024)

- **Title:** "Can Large-Language Models Replace Humans in Agile Effort Estimation? Lessons from a Controlled Experiment"
- **Authors:** Luka Pavlic, Vasilka Saklamaeva, Tina Beranic
- **Publication:** Applied Sciences (MDPI), 14(24), 12006
- **Published:** December 22, 2024
- **URL:** https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/14/24/12006

### Methodology

Controlled experiment where development teams upgraded an existing information system:
- Experimental group: used **GitLab Duo** for estimation
- Control group: used conventional methods (planning poker, analogy-based planning)

### Key Results

- Generative-AI-based estimation tools achieved only **16% accuracy** -- insufficient for industry standards
- AI tools offered valuable support for **task breakdown and iteration planning** even when point estimates were poor
- Participants recommended combining conventional methods with AI-based tools for enhanced accuracy

### Caveats

- Single tool tested (GitLab Duo), limiting generalizability
- Limited empirical data / small-scale project
- Published December 2024; AI estimation tools have evolved since

## Source 5: Bui et al. -- LLM Multi-Agent Framework for Agile Estimation (arXiv 2025)

- **Title:** "An LLM-based multi-agent framework for agile effort estimation"
- **Authors:** Thanh-Long Bui, Hoa Khanh Dam, Rashina Hoda
- **Submitted:** September 17, 2025
- **URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.14483

### Framework: SEEAgent (Multi-Agent Estimation)

Novel approach where multiple LLM agents and human team members collaborate on estimation:
- Each agent and human shares perspectives and assumptions on user story complexity
- If estimates diverge, agents and humans discuss reasons for differences
- Agents re-estimate based on discussion, producing consensus-based estimates
- Leverages collective team knowledge and reduces individual biases

### Results

- Outperforms state-of-the-art ML techniques across evaluation metrics on real-life datasets
- Human study with practitioners showed "overwhelmingly positive experience"
- 75% of practitioners did not object to relying on agent estimates
- Only one-third thought the agent could fully substitute a human expert
- More than half trusted the agent's estimations

### Estimation Relevance

This is the most directly applicable framework for teams wanting to use LLMs as estimation participants (not just estimation targets). It models the planning poker / consensus process with AI agents.

### Caveats

- Preprint (not yet peer-reviewed as of search date)
- Evaluation on historical datasets, not live sprint planning
- Does not address estimation of AI-assisted *implementation* tasks specifically

## Source 6: Ashish Agarwal -- "New Rules for Estimating Software Development Time in AI-era"

- **Author:** Ashish Agarwal
- **Publication Date:** May 2, 2025
- **URL:** https://toashishagarwal.medium.com/new-rules-for-estimating-software-development-time-in-ai-era-460ec5347e1a

### Proposed Framework Elements

1. **Shift in Effort Distribution:** AI shifts effort from coding to requirements analysis, prompt engineering, and validation of AI-generated outputs
2. **AI Augmentation Factor (AAF) for COCOMO:** Proposes extending COCOMO with an AAF ranging from 0.5 to 1.5, where lower values reflect higher AI productivity. Multiplied into the effort equation alongside the existing Effort Adjustment Factor (EAF). *Note: Agarwal attributes this to "a 2023 study" but does not provide a specific citation. The original source could not be independently verified.*
3. **New Estimation Parameters:**
   - Tool proficiency (team's experience with AI tools)
   - Task suitability (how well a task maps to AI capabilities)
   - Output quality (reliability of AI-generated artifacts)
4. **Task Categorization by AI Impact:**
   - **High-impact tasks** (CRUD operations, testing, boilerplate): significant AI acceleration
   - **Low-impact tasks** (architecture, debugging, stakeholder communication): minimal AI benefit
5. **AI-Aware Three-Point Estimation:**
   - AI Optimistic (AO)
   - AI Realistic (AR)
   - Traditional/Realistic (TR)
6. **Productivity variation by developer level:**
   - Junior developers: up to 300% improvement on routine tasks
   - Senior developers on complex integration: marginal improvement

### Caveats

- Medium blog post, not peer-reviewed
- AAF attribution to "a 2023 study" is unverifiable -- treat as the author's proposed framework
- Specific numbers (20-50% reduction, 300% junior improvement) cite McKinsey 2023 but without precise reference links

## Source 10: Scrum.org Forum Discussion on Story Points with AI Tools

- **Title:** "How to approach story point estimation with advent of AI Dev acceleration tools?"
- **URL:** https://www.scrum.org/forum/scrum-forum/94752/how-approach-story-point-estimation-advent-ai-dev-acceleration-tools

### Community Recommendations

1. **Estimate complexity, not time:** Story points should measure complexity, and complexity doesn't change based on who (or what) implements the work. "A story that touches 5 services and handles 3 edge cases is still more complex than one that updates a single field, regardless of who (or what) implements it."
2. **Keep estimation tool-agnostic:** "Who does the work (Person A, Person B or AI for that matter) should not be a criteria for assigning story points."
3. **Use empirical velocity:** Monitor sprint-by-sprint and understand your new average velocity, then use it for planning
4. **Consider moving beyond story points:** Measure throughput and cycle time instead, with emphasis on smallest valuable units of work

### Caveats

- Forum discussion, not formal research
- Multiple community voices, not a single authored framework
- Represents practitioner consensus rather than validated methodology

## Source 18: ACONIC Framework -- Formal Task Complexity for LLMs

- **Title:** "An Approach for Systematic Decomposition of Complex LLM Tasks"
- **URL:** https://arxiv.org/html/2510.07772v1

### Framework

ACONIC (Analysis of CONstraint-Induced Complexity) provides a formal, mathematical approach to measuring task complexity for LLMs:
- Models tasks as constraint satisfaction problems (3-SAT)
- Uses constraint graph properties (graph size, treewidth) as complexity measures
- Defines "frontiers of difficulty" separating tasks easily solvable by LLMs from those requiring structural decomposition

### Estimation Relevance

Could provide a principled basis for classifying tasks as "AI-suitable" vs. "needs human intervention" based on formal complexity measures rather than heuristics.

### New Estimation Units or Scales Proposed for AI-Augmented Development

**Finding: No new estimation units have been formally proposed.** The field has not produced a replacement for story points, ideal days, or function points that is specific to AI-assisted development. Instead, the trend is toward:
- Keeping complexity-based estimation (story points) unchanged
- Adding new measurement dimensions (AI-assisted commits, prompt-to-commit success rate, acceptance rate)
- Tracking outcome metrics (throughput, cycle time) rather than effort estimates

### Task Decomposition Approaches Accounting for LLM Capability Boundaries

1. **ACONIC** (arXiv 2510.07772) -- formal complexity measurement using constraint satisfaction; defines mathematical frontiers separating LLM-solvable from LLM-unsolvable tasks
2. **ADaPT** (Allen AI) -- adaptive decomposition that recursively breaks down tasks when the LLM fails, adapting to both task complexity and LLM capability
3. **TDAG** -- dynamic decomposition with generated sub-agents; addresses error propagation in fixed decomposition plans
4. **Osmani's 70/30 Rule** -- practical heuristic: AI handles 70% (scaffolding), humans handle 30% (edge cases, security, integration)
5. **Agarwal's Task Segmentation** -- split projects into AI-suitable and non-AI-suitable tasks, estimate each separately

### The Effort Shift Problem

Multiple sources converge on the same observation: AI shifts the bottleneck from implementation to context preparation, review, and verification.

- **Osmani:** "Code review is becoming the new bottleneck"
- **Agarwal:** "AI shifts effort from coding to requirements analysis, prompt engineering, and validation"
- **Kmino:** "AI-generated code requires expanded review scope" that "typically requires more time than traditional code review"
- **METR:** AI introduced "extra cognitive load and context-switching" that disrupted productivity
- **DORA:** Without organizational maturity in version control, data ecosystems, and platform engineering, AI-accelerated coding creates operational risk

## Gaps and Open Questions

1. **No standardized framework exists** for estimating AI-assisted development work. Every organization is developing bespoke heuristics.
2. **No new estimation unit** has been proposed to replace or supplement story points for AI-augmented teams.
3. **The perception gap** (developers think AI helps more than it does) has been demonstrated only for experienced developers on mature codebases. The gap may differ for junior developers or greenfield projects.
4. **Context preparation effort** -- the time spent preparing prompts, specs, and context for LLMs -- is not measured or estimated by any existing framework.
5. **Verification overhead** -- the time spent reviewing, testing, and fixing AI-generated code -- lacks standardized estimation approaches.
6. **Model capability evolution** -- estimation frameworks become obsolete as AI tools improve, with no mechanism for dynamic recalibration.
7. **The AAF for COCOMO** lacks a verified original source and has not been standardized.
8. **Multi-agent estimation frameworks** (like SEEAgent) show promise but have only been evaluated on historical datasets, not in live sprint planning.
